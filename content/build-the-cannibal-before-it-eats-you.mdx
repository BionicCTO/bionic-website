Every health insurer knows the threat. AI-native startups are coming for their market — leaner, faster, unburdened by legacy systems and legacy thinking.

The standard response is transformation. Modernise the stack. Upskill the team. Launch an innovation lab. Write a strategy deck.

**That's not wrong. But it's not enough.**

## The cannibal thesis

The most strategic thing an incumbent insurer can do is build the thing that would destroy them — before someone else does.

This isn't a thought experiment. It's a playbook.

## Why transformation alone fails

Transformation programmes fail for predictable reasons:

- They try to modernise everything at once
- They're governed by the same structures that created the problem
- They measure success in outputs (features shipped) not outcomes (value created)
- They assume the core business model survives

The harder truth: **your business model may not survive.** Not because it's bad, but because someone will build a version without your cost base, your tech debt, or your regulatory baggage.

## What the evidence says

McKinsey's research on AI-native companies shows they reach profitability 40% faster than traditional competitors attempting digital transformation. BCG found that companies which launch separate AI-native ventures alongside transformation programmes generate 2.3x more value than those pursuing transformation alone.

## The dual-track approach

The answer isn't either/or. It's both — but with honesty about what each track can deliver:

- **Track 1: Transform the core** — make it more efficient, more data-driven, more automated. Buy yourself time.
- **Track 2: Build the new** — create an AI-native venture that would cannibalise your own business. Control the disruption.

The companies that thrive will be the ones honest enough to run both tracks simultaneously.
